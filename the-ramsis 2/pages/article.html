The Architectural Shift of Digital Geopolitics.

How infrastructure defines the next era of sovereign power in an increasingly fragmented global network. We examine the cables, nodes, and protocols that shape the new world order.

A
Alistair Thorne
October 14, 2024 · 12 min read

The map of power has always been drawn in infrastructure. Roman roads, Venetian trade routes, British railway networks — each epoch's hegemon stamped its authority not in flags, but in the physical architecture of movement and communication.

Today's contest is no different. But the cables are buried beneath oceans, the nodes are inside climate-controlled data centers, and the protocols are written by engineers who may not grasp — or care — about the geopolitical weight of their choices.

The Chokepoints Nobody Sees

There are fewer than 500 submarine cable systems currently operational, and they carry more than 95% of international internet traffic. The majority of those cables — the ones that matter most — converge on a handful of landing stations. Destroy or compromise three or four of them, and you don't just disrupt the internet. You effectively sever the financial system of entire continents.

"The undersea cable is the most important piece of infrastructure most people have never thought about."

What's striking is how few actors control these chokepoints. The same handful of technology companies — principally American, with growing Chinese presence — own or co-own the majority of new cable capacity. Their corporate decisions about routing, redundancy, and access are, whether they intend it or not, decisions about geopolitical risk.

The Protocol Wars

Infrastructure is not only physical. The standards that govern how data moves across networks are equally consequential. And increasingly, the bodies that set those standards are contested terrain.

The International Telecommunication Union, historically dominated by Western democracies, has seen a sustained effort from state-aligned actors to reshape global technical standards in ways that permit — and even facilitate — greater state surveillance and control of internet traffic.
